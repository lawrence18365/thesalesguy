<p>I never hear sales trainers or managers talk about how important business acumen is when it comes to prospecting.</p>

<p>And that is a problem.</p>

<p>Any good cold caller knows the purpose of calling is to find prospects with problems they fix.</p>

<p>But often prospects are unaware of their problems.</p>

<div class="section-divider"></div>

<h2>Cold Calling Business Acumen Is What Spots the Real Problem</h2>

<p>Most salespeople hear an objection and take it at face value.</p>

<p>They call a prospect. The prospect says something that sounds like a reason not to meet. The salesperson accepts it, updates the CRM, and moves on.</p>

<p>That is not qualifying. That is just letting the prospect end the call for you.</p>

<p>A good salesperson listens for what is underneath the answer. Not in a manipulative way. In a business way.</p>

<p>Because prospects do not always describe problems clearly. Sometimes they describe the thing that is working, while completely missing the thing that is costing them time, money, speed, or quality.</p>

<h2>The Recruiter Example Every SDR Should Understand</h2>

<p>Let's say you're a recruiter, calling a company that has an ad on Seek.</p>

<p>You call a prospect and they say:</p>

<p><em>"We get loads of applications via Seek. The last job we put up got 100 applicants."</em></p>

<p>Most recruiters will accept it and think:</p>

<p><em>"This company doesn't need us."</em></p>

<p>See quantity is not a problem here.</p>

<p>But time could be.</p>

<p>A good salesperson will realise this and shine a light on the issue. To do this, you need to understand business.</p>

<div class="article-pullquote">
    "Wow, 100, that's a lot, well done. Typically when we see such high applicants, most of them are unqualified or overseas, and it can take hours to go through. What are you doing to make sure that you find the golden nuggets and aren't spending too much time on irrelevant candidates?"
</div>

<p>The recruiter has highlighted a potential problem.</p>

<p>And more importantly, one the prospect may not have considered.</p>

<p>The good applicants will have applied elsewhere. The prospect would want to reach out to them as quickly as possible. That is not easy with that much volume.</p>

<p>Now the conversation has moved.</p>

<p>It is no longer "do you need help finding applicants?"</p>

<p>It is "are you able to find the right applicants quickly enough before someone else does?"</p>

<p>That is a completely different conversation.</p>

<h2>Why Scripts Alone Do Not Fix Prospecting</h2>

<p>Cold calling is often given to people fresh out of uni.</p>

<p>Business acumen is not something they have. For most, it is their first B2B job.</p>

<p>So what happens?</p>

<p>They get handed scripts. They get taught <a href="/blog/why-cold-call-openers-sound-like-begging/">fancy cold call openers</a>. They get told to use pattern interrupts. They get measured on dials and meetings.</p>

<p>But they are not taught how businesses actually break.</p>

<p>They are not taught how a problem in one area creates cost somewhere else. They are not taught how to listen for time waste, risk, speed, quality, poor conversion, missed opportunities, or the impact of doing nothing.</p>

<p>So they miss the problem.</p>

<p>Then the manager says cold calling does not work.</p>

<p>No. Your team just did not understand the business well enough to have a business conversation.</p>

<h2>Business Problems Drive Conversations</h2>

<p>While most sales trainers are spending their time coming up with pattern interrupts or teaching regurgitated scripts, my clients are learning how to maximise each conversation from understanding real business.</p>

<p>That is the difference between a cold call that dies after thirty seconds and a cold call that turns into a qualified opportunity.</p>

<p>The goal is not to force a meeting. I have written before about why <a href="/blog/cold-call-training-getting-a-meeting-wrong-goal/">getting a meeting is the wrong goal</a> when the prospect has not admitted there is a problem.</p>

<p>The goal is to find out whether a real business problem exists, then help the prospect see it clearly enough to care.</p>

<p>That requires questions. It requires listening. It requires knowing what problems actually look like in the prospect's world.</p>

<p>Business problems drive conversations. Leave no stone unturned.</p>
