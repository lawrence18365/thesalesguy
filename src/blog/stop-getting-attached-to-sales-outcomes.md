<p>Don't get attached to the outcome.</p>

<p>Salespeople hear this all the time.</p>

<p>And yet time and time again, salespeople are attached.</p>

<p>What happens when they become attached?</p>

<p>They push. They beg. They discount. They convince. They come across needy.</p>

<p>And that is not what prospects want.</p>

<div class="section-divider"></div>

<h2>Attachment Makes Salespeople Push for the Meeting</h2>

<p>Let's pretend a prospect says:</p>

<p><em>"We are working with your competitor."</em></p>

<p>The attached salesperson says:</p>

<p><em>"Yeah ok, well it's always good to compare, and we have been known to come in cheaper. I suggest we book a meeting so I can show you why people choose us."</em></p>

<p>Pushy.</p>

<p>Needy.</p>

<p>Convincing.</p>

<p>If the prospect agrees to a meeting, it is likely they will not show.</p>

<p>Because they were not interested. They were escaping.</p>

<p>That is how salespeople end up with calendars full of ghosts and call it pipeline.</p>

<h2>The Unattached Salesperson Does Something Different</h2>

<p>The unattached salesperson does not rush for the diary.</p>

<p>They work out how to get there.</p>

<p>Same objection:</p>

<p><em>"We are working with your competitor."</em></p>

<p>Better response:</p>

<p><em>"Yea, was expecting you to say that. If there was one thing you wish their service or product did better, what would that look like?"</em></p>

<p>The prospect says customer service.</p>

<p>You ask what they mean.</p>

<p>They explain the support team is slow because it is overseas.</p>

<p>Now you have something real.</p>

<p>Not a meeting. A problem.</p>

<p>This is the same principle behind <a href="/blog/cold-call-training-handle-competitor-objection/">handling the competitor objection</a>. You do not overcome it. You understand it.</p>

<h2>Give the Prospect Control</h2>

<p>Once the prospect has explained the issue, you can ask:</p>

<div class="article-pullquote">
    "If I showed you a similar product, and by the end of me showing you, you believed it offered superior customer service while not compromising quality, what would you do?"
</div>

<p>If they say they would be interested, the meeting flows naturally.</p>

<p>You did not push.</p>

<p>You did not beg.</p>

<p>You did not go straight to price.</p>

<p>You let the prospect convince themselves.</p>

<p>That is what detachment looks like in the real world.</p>

<h2>Wanting the Outcome Makes You Lose Control</h2>

<p>If you're making cold calls, the minute you want an outcome is the minute you lose control.</p>

<p>You stop listening.</p>

<p>You start hunting for any path to a meeting.</p>

<p>You accept weak answers because you want the booking.</p>

<p>Then the prospect does not show, the AE complains, and the manager tells everyone to make more calls.</p>

<p>The better way is simple.</p>

<p>Detach from the meeting. Attach to the problem.</p>

<p>If there is a real problem, the meeting will make sense. If there is not, move on.</p>
